Pink Figurehead - Logarithmic Function

Transformations

I took this photo in a makeup shop at Sunway Pyramid. I chose this photo because it is my favourite makeup shop in Malaysia. I really enjoy makeup and buying new products there, so I always visit this shop whenever I go to Sunway Pyramid.

To model the pink figurehead appearing in the photograph, I first identified that the shape closely resembles a logarithmic function. Therefore, I selected the parent function \( y=\log(x) \) as the starting point.

From observing the orientation of the curve in the image, I noticed that the graph decreases from left to right rather than increasing. This indicated a reflection across the y-axis, so I modified the function to \( y=\log(-x) \) to match the direction of the graph.

Initially, I found it difficult to determine the correct transformation because the visible part of the curve was very small and looked different from the parent logarithmic graph. Since the graph appeared very flat and was positioned away from the original parent function, it was difficult to directly compare the two shapes.

After further analysis, I realised that the function had undergone several transformations. The negative sign inside the logarithm represents a reflection across the y-axis, causing the graph to decrease from left to right.

Next, I analysed the leading coefficient \(0.1\). Since there is no negative sign before the coefficient, there is no reflection across the x-axis. Because \(0.1\) is less than \(1\), the graph undergoes a vertical compression. This transformation makes the graph significantly flatter than the parent function.

I experimented with many different values in order to closely match the curve shown in the photograph. Larger values than \(0.1\) made the graph too steep, while smaller values flattened the graph excessively. Therefore, \(0.1\) produced the best fit for the photograph.

After determining the overall shape, I applied translations to move the graph into the correct position. I shifted the graph horizontally to the right so that the vertical asymptote aligned with its position in the image. Then, I applied a vertical translation upward so the curve passed through important points in the photograph.

The graph initially appeared too low, so I translated it upward by \(1.2\) units. This resulted in the final function:

\[ y=0.1\log\bigl(-(x-0.6)+1.2\bigr) \]

This equation closely models the shape shown in the photograph.

Domain and Range

The domain of my function is:

\[ D=\{x\in\mathbb{R}\mid 0.43\le x\le 0.58\} \]

I could not fit the equation to the shape in the photograph while including the entire range of x-values, so I restricted the domain to the interval that best matched the visible curve.

The parent function \(y=\log(x)\) has the domain:

\[ x>0 \]

and the range:

\[ y\in\mathbb{R} \]

This means the function is defined only for positive x-values and can produce any real number. The parent logarithmic function also has a vertical asymptote at \(x=0\), where the function is undefined.

After applying transformations, my function also has a vertical asymptote. However, the restricted interval used in my model is relatively far from the asymptote, so the discontinuity does not affect the part of the graph being analysed.

Since the function is continuous on the restricted interval, the range can be determined by evaluating the function at the endpoints of the domain. By substituting \(x=0.43\) and \(x=0.58\), I obtained approximate y-values of \(1.12\) and \(1.03\).

Therefore, the approximate range is:

\[ R=\{y\in\mathbb{R}\mid 1.03\le y\le 1.12\} \]

Roof - Quadratic Reciprocal Function

Transformations

I took this photo at a park in Malaysia. This photograph is very memorable and important to me because my family came to Malaysia to visit me, and we spent time walking around together. Whenever I look at this photo, many happy memories come flooding back.

To model the roof appearing in the photograph, I first identified that the shape closely resembles a quadratic reciprocal function. Therefore, I selected the parent function \( y=\frac{1}{x^2} \) as the starting point.

From observing the curve in the image, I noticed that the graph had a similar overall shape to the reciprocal function, with two curves approaching a vertical asymptote. However, the graph appeared much wider than the parent function, which indicated that a vertical compression was required.

Initially, I found it difficult to determine the correct compression ratio and compare the shapes accurately. To simplify the modelling process, I focused first on matching the overall shape of the graph before applying translations.

When comparing the graph with the parent function, I noticed how quickly the edges moved away from the asymptotes. Through experimentation and observation in Desmos, I determined that the graph required significant vertical compression. After testing different coefficients, I found that the factor \( \frac{1.8}{100} \) produced the closest fit to the roof in the photograph.

I also noticed that the graph did not approach the x-axis directly. Instead, adding a constant term to the denominator adjusted the width and shape of the curve. Therefore, I added \(3\) to the denominator, which helped the function better match the roof structure and prevented the function from becoming undefined near the translated asymptote.

After determining the overall shape, I applied translations to position the graph correctly. I shifted the graph \(0.6\) units to the right, resulting in the term \( (x-0.6)^2 \).

Then, I translated the graph upward by \(1.5\) units so that the curve aligned more closely with the visible section of the roof.

The final function obtained was:

\[ y=\frac{1.8}{100(x-0.6)^2+3}+1.5 \]

This equation closely models the shape shown in the photograph.

Summary of Transformations

Parent function:

\[ y=\frac{1}{x^2} \]

Vertical compression by a factor of \(1.8\):

\[ y=\frac{1.8}{x^2} \]

Horizontal compression caused by the factor of \(100\):

\[ y=\frac{1.8}{100x^2} \]

Addition of \(+3\) in the denominator:

\[ y=\frac{1.8}{100x^2+3} \]

Horizontal translation \(0.6\) units to the right:

\[ y=\frac{1.8}{100(x-0.6)^2+3} \]

Vertical translation \(1.5\) units upward:

\[ y=\frac{1.8}{100(x-0.6)^2+3}+1.5 \]

Domain and Range

The domain of my function is restricted to the visible portion of the roof shown in the photograph:

\[ D=\{x\in\mathbb{R}\mid 0.367\le x\le 0.85\} \]

I limited the domain because only part of the roof was visible in the image. If I had taken the photograph from a closer distance, the roof would have appeared larger, allowing the graph to fit the structure more accurately. Therefore, my model only represents the visible section captured in the photograph.

The parent function \( y=\frac{1}{x^2} \) has the domain:

\[ D=\{x\in\mathbb{R}\mid x\ne0\} \]

and the range:

\[ R=\{y\in\mathbb{R}\mid y>0\} \]

This means the function is defined for all real numbers except \(x=0\), and the output values are always positive. The graph also has a vertical asymptote at \(x=0\), where the function is undefined.

After applying transformations, my function also contains an asymptotic feature. However, since the restricted interval \(0.367\le x\le0.85\) is completely away from the asymptote, the undefined behaviour does not affect my model.

Since the function is continuous on the restricted interval, the range can be determined by evaluating the function at the endpoints of the domain. By substituting \(x=0.367\) and \(x=0.85\), I obtained approximate output values of \(1.71\) and \(1.695\).

Therefore, the approximate range of the function is:

\[ R=\{y\in\mathbb{R}\mid 1.695\le y\le1.71\} \]

My Friends Memory - Linear Reciprocal Function

Transformations

This photo was taken last Christmas at a photo booth store inside Sunway Pyramid. My friends and I often take pictures there because photo strips are very meaningful to us. They help preserve memories and remind us of the fun times we spend together. I chose this image because the curved border between the photo sections closely resembled a reciprocal function, making it a suitable real-life example for mathematical modelling.

To model the curved border in the centre of the photo strip, I first identified that the shape closely resembled the parent function \( y=\frac{1}{x} \).

From observing the image, I noticed that the curve became very steep near the vertical axis and gradually flattened as it moved away from the centre. This matched the general behaviour of a reciprocal function with vertical and horizontal asymptotes.

Initially, the parent function appeared much wider than the curve shown in the image. To make the graph narrower and steeper, I applied a horizontal compression by multiplying the x-value by \(10\) inside the denominator. This transformed the function into:

\[ y=\frac{1}{10x} \]

Next, I applied a horizontal translation by adding \(0.1\) inside the denominator. This shifted the graph slightly to the left so that the vertical asymptote aligned more closely with the centre curve in the image.

Finally, I translated the graph upward by \(2.9\) units so that the curve matched the position of the border in the photograph.

This resulted in the final equation:

\[ y=\frac{1}{10x+0.1}+2.9 \]

This equation closely models the shape shown in the photograph.

Summary of Transformations

Parent function:

\[ y=\frac{1}{x} \]

Horizontal compression by a factor of \(10\):

\[ y=\frac{1}{10x} \]

Horizontal translation slightly left:

\[ y=\frac{1}{10x+0.1} \]

Vertical translation upward by \(2.9\) units:

\[ y=\frac{1}{10x+0.1}+2.9 \]

Domain and Range

The domain of the function is:

\[ x\in(-\infty,-0.01)\cup(-0.01,\infty) \]

The graph cannot include \(x=-0.01\) because this value makes the denominator equal to zero, causing the function to become undefined. This creates a vertical asymptote near the centre of the image. Although the two branches appear very close visually, they never actually connect mathematically.

The parent reciprocal function \( y=\frac{1}{x} \) has a domain of all real numbers except \(x=0\), and a range of all real numbers except \(y=0\). Therefore, the original function contains both a vertical asymptote and a horizontal asymptote.

After applying the transformations, the vertical asymptote shifted from \(x=0\) to \(x=-0.01\), while the horizontal asymptote shifted upward from \(y=0\) to \(y=2.9\).

Therefore, the range of the function is:

\[ y\in(-\infty,2.9)\cup(2.9,\infty) \]

The graph approaches \(y=2.9\) but never actually reaches it because reciprocal functions cannot equal their horizontal asymptotes.

Humberger Shop - Polynomial Function of Degree 4

Summary of Transformations

This photo was taken on the street opposite Sunway College. There are many restaurants on this street, including Wallace. This place is meaningful to me because my friends and I often come here to eat lunch after school.

I chose this photo because the Wallace logo on the sign has a very recognizable “W” shape that could be modelled using a polynomial function of degree 4. I first identified that the graph had four x-intercepts and was symmetric about the y-axis, which suggested that a factored quartic equation would be the best model. Therefore, I selected the parent function \( y=x^4 \) as my starting point.

From observing the logo carefully, I noticed that the graph crossed the x-axis four times at approximately \(x=-0.7\), \(x=-0.2\), \(x=0.2\), and \(x=0.7\). Therefore, I introduced these four x-intercepts into the equation, producing:

\[ y=(x+0.7)(x+0.2)(x-0.2)(x-0.7) \]

Initially, the graph appeared too wide and too flat compared to the logo. To solve this problem, I applied a vertical stretch by multiplying the equation by \(12\). I experimented with several values in Desmos and observed how the width and steepness changed. A factor of \(12\) produced a graph that was narrower and taller, allowing it to fit the logo much more accurately.

This resulted in the final equation:

\[ y=12(x+0.7)(x+0.2)(x-0.2)(x-0.7) \]

This equation closely models the shape shown in the photograph.

Summary of Transformations

Parent function:

\[ y=x^4 \]

Four x-intercepts added at \(x=-0.7\), \(x=-0.2\), \(x=0.2\), and \(x=0.7\):

\[ y=(x+0.7)(x+0.2)(x-0.2)(x-0.7) \]

Vertical stretch by a factor of \(12\):

\[ y=12(x+0.7)(x+0.2)(x-0.2)(x-0.7) \]

Domain and Range

The domain of my polynomial model is:

\[ -0.9 \le x \le 0.9 \]

I restricted the domain because only the middle section of the graph is visible in the Wallace logo. Outside this interval, the graph increases very quickly upward and no longer matches the shape shown in the photograph. Therefore, my model is only valid for the visible portion of the logo.

The parent function \( y=x^4 \) has a domain of all real numbers and a range of:

\[ y\ge0 \]

This means the original quartic function extends infinitely in both horizontal directions and never produces negative outputs.

After applying the transformations, the graph still extends infinitely upward. However, because the middle sections of the graph dip below the x-axis, the range now includes negative values.

The minimum value of the graph is approximately:

\[ y\approx-1 \]

which occurs near the valleys of the “W” shape.

Therefore, the approximate range of the function is:

\[ -1\le y<\infty \]

Plush Shark Toy – Radical Semicircle Function

Transformations

I chose this photograph because the rounded top of the plush shark toy closely resembled the shape of a semicircle. The smooth upward arc suggested that the curve could be modelled using a radical function derived from the equation of a circle.

The standard equation of a circle is:

\[ (x-h)^2+(y-k)^2=r^2 \]

where \((h,k)\) represents the centre of the circle and \(r\) represents the radius.

A full circle is not considered a function because a single x-value can correspond to two different y-values, which fails the vertical line test. To create a valid function, I isolated only the upper half of the circle by taking the positive square root.

Rearranging the circle equation to solve for \(y\) gives:

\[ (x-h)^2+(y-k)^2=r^2 \]

\[ (y-k)^2=r^2-(x-h)^2 \]

\[ y-k=\sqrt{r^2-(x-h)^2} \]

\[ y=\sqrt{r^2-(x-h)^2}+k \]

This produces the parent radical semicircle function. Since every x-value now corresponds to only one y-value, the graph satisfies the vertical line test and can be treated as a valid function.

To fit the curve to the rounded top of the plush shark toy, I applied several transformations to the parent equation. First, I adjusted the centre of the circle to \((1.4,8.5)\) so that the graph aligned with the middle of the toy.

Next, I selected a radius of \(145\), which controlled the size and curvature of the semicircle. Finally, I applied a vertical adjustment of \(3.4\) units to fine-tune the height of the graph so that it matched the visible curve in the photograph more accurately.

The final function obtained was:

\[ y=3.4+\sqrt{145-(x-1.4)^2}+8.5 \]

This equation closely models the rounded top of the plush shark toy shown in the photograph.

Domain and Range

To model only the visible section of the plush shark toy, I restricted the domain to the interval where the curve appears in the photograph.

The horizontal width of the visible arc extends approximately from \(x=-15\) to \(x=15\). Therefore, the domain is:

\[ D=\{x\in\mathbb{R}\mid -15\le x\le15\} \]

Since the function is continuous on the restricted interval, the range can be determined by evaluating the endpoints and the highest point of the semicircle.

The maximum value occurs at the vertex of the graph, while the minimum values occur near the endpoints of the domain. This gives an approximate range of:

\[ R=\{y\in\mathbb{R}\mid 8.5\le y\le20.5\} \]

Unlike reciprocal or logarithmic functions, this semicircle model does not contain any asymptotes. The graph remains continuous throughout the restricted interval, making it suitable for modelling the smooth curved shape of the plush shark toy.

The Unique Book - Trigonometric Function with an Asymptote

Transformations

I chose this image because the wave pattern on the book cover reminded me of trigonometric graphs. The repeating curves and asymptotic behaviour closely resemble a cosecant function, so I selected the parent function \(y=\csc(x)\) as my starting point.

The graph opens downward rather than upward, so I applied a reflection in the x-axis. This produced a negative coefficient, giving a vertically reflected cosecant curve.

Next, I adjusted the period by modifying the inside coefficient. By experimenting in Desmos, I found that a horizontal scaling factor was needed to match the spacing of the repeating waves.

After refining the shape, I applied translations to position the graph over the image correctly. The final model became:

\[ y=-10.6\,\csc\bigl(-0.5(x+1.27)\bigr)+2.3 \]

This transformation includes reflection, vertical stretch, horizontal scaling, and translations.

Summary of Transformations

• Vertical stretch by a factor of 10.6
• Horizontal stretch/compression due to coefficient -0.5
• Horizontal translation of 1.27 units
• Vertical translation of 2.3 units

Domain and Range

\[ D=\{x\in\mathbb{R}\mid 7.4 < x < 14.5\} \]

The parent function \(y=\csc(x)\) is undefined when \(\sin(x)=0\), which creates vertical asymptotes at \(x=n\pi\), where \(n\in\mathbb{Z}\).

Its range is:

\[ R=\{y\in\mathbb{R}\mid y\le -1 \text{ or } y\ge 1\} \]

After transformations and restricting the domain, only a visible portion of the curve is modelled. Evaluating the endpoints gives approximate values, producing the range:

\[ R\approx \{y\in\mathbb{R}\mid 68.90 < y < 82.42\} \]

I also restricted the domain so that only the visible portion of the curve appeared on the graph. This allowed the function to model the mirror accurately without extending beyond the photographed section.

The final function obtained was:

\[ y=33^{1.3(x-1.06)} \]

My arms-absolute value function

Transformations

I took this photo with my friend at my study table in Sunway College. This table holds many memories of my academic journey, including long hours of revision, assignment preparation, and exam study sessions. It represents both hard work and personal growth.

To model the shape of my arms shown in the photograph, I identified that the curve closely resembles an absolute value function. Therefore, I selected the parent function \( y = |x| \) as the starting point.

The graph formed a V-shape, which confirmed that an absolute value model was appropriate. However, compared to the parent function, the shape appeared slightly steeper and narrower. This indicated a vertical stretch, so I applied a factor of \(1.1\), giving: \( y = 1.1|x| \).

Next, I observed that the graph was also slightly compressed horizontally. To adjust this, I applied a horizontal scaling factor of \(0.8\) inside the absolute value, producing: \( y = 1.1|0.8x| \).

Finally, I translated the graph so it aligned correctly with the position of my arms in the image. I shifted it \(0.015\) units to the right and \(0.225\) units upward, resulting in the final model:

\[ y = 1.1|0.8(x - 0.015)| + 0.225 \]

This function closely matches the shape and position of my arms in the photograph.

Summary of Transformations

• Vertical stretch: \( y = |x| \rightarrow y = 1.1|x| \)
• Horizontal scaling: \( y = 1.1|x| \rightarrow y = 1.1|0.8x| \)
• Horizontal translation: \( y = 1.1|0.8x| \rightarrow y = 1.1|0.8(x - 0.015)| \)
• Vertical translation: \( y = 1.1|0.8(x - 0.015)| \rightarrow y = 1.1|0.8(x - 0.015)| + 0.225 \)

Domain and Range

The domain of my function is:

\[ D = \{x \in \mathbb{R} \mid -0.36 \le x \le 0.15\} \]

I restricted the domain to match the visible section of my arms in the photograph. Because the photo only captures part of the full shape, the model is valid only within this interval.

The parent function \( y = |x| \) has:

\[ D = \mathbb{R}, \quad R = \{y \in \mathbb{R} \mid y \ge 0\} \]

It is defined for all real numbers and produces only non-negative values. It has no asymptotes because it is continuous everywhere.

Since the function is continuous on the restricted domain, the range can be found by evaluating the endpoints. Substituting \(x = -0.36\) and \(x = 0.15\) gives approximately: \(y \approx 0.558\) and \(y \approx 0.3405\).

Therefore, the range is:

\[ R = \{y \in \mathbb{R} \mid 0.3405 \le y \le 0.558\} \]

Peace Sign - Cosine Function

Transformations

I chose this photograph because the repeating vertical motion of the peace sign gesture closely resembled a cosine graph. The smooth oscillating movement matched the repeating pattern of a trigonometric function. Therefore, I selected the parent function \( y=\cos(x) \) as the starting point.

To fit the scale and position of the gesture in the photograph, I applied several transformations to the parent cosine function.

First, I applied a vertical compression with an amplitude of \(0.8\). Since the amplitude is less than \(1\), the graph becomes vertically compressed, which better matched the smaller range of motion of my hand in the image.

Next, I adjusted the period using the coefficient \(-9.5\) inside the cosine function. This caused a horizontal compression, making the wave repeat more quickly to match the fast motion of the gesture. The negative sign also reflects the graph across the y-axis, allowing the direction of the wave to align more accurately with the photograph.

I then translated the graph horizontally by \(1.245\) units to the right so that the peak of the cosine curve aligned with the position of my hand.

Finally, I translated the graph upward by \(1.53\) units so that the curve matched the vertical position of my hand relative to the origin.

The final transformed function is:

\[ y=0.8\cos\bigl(-9.5(x-1.245)\bigr)+1.53 \]

This equation closely models the repeating motion of the peace sign gesture shown in the photograph.

Domain and Range

The domain of the function is restricted to the visible section of the gesture captured in the photograph. Based on the Desmos grid, the relevant interval is:

\[ D=\{x\in\mathbb{R}\mid 0.207\le x\le1\} \]

This restriction means the model is only intended to represent the visible portion of the wave shown in the image.

The parent cosine function \( y=\cos(x) \) has the range:

\[ R=\{y\in\mathbb{R}\mid -1\le y\le1\} \]

After applying the transformations, the amplitude became \(0.8\) and the graph was shifted upward by \(1.53\) units. Therefore, the new range is:

\[ R=\{y\in\mathbb{R}\mid 1.53-0.8\le y\le1.53+0.8\} \]

Simplifying gives:

\[ R=\{y\in\mathbb{R}\mid 0.73\le y\le2.33\} \]

This range matches the vertical movement of my hand in the photograph, from its lowest point at \(0.73\) to its highest point at \(2.33\).
